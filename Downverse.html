DOWNLOADING DOWNVERSE - A UNIVERSE.TORRENT


To read Nikki Reimer's DOWNVERSE (Talonbooks, 2014) is to download a torrent file. Hyperconnected language swarms the page. Bytes like “plastic give pink give pictures the he the split” are like examining an in-progress transfer - most of the information may be there, but it is as yet unsorted (9). Formed, but yet unfed through any rigorous systemization - the poem steals glimpses before the “Big [br]Other” (as one poem is titled) begins watching, fleetingly before its imposition. (31). It is the poetry of a browser with twenty tabs open.

Such glimpses continue too in the form of flickering channels in “television vs. the real”, a poem that switches phrase by phrase between registers of postmodern critical theory - drawing upon such Lacanian apercus as “what lengths men go to make Woman exist” (26) - and fuzzier, more placid linguistic forms like “we tried to watch jPod but it had been cancelled” and “we were watching Ellen / & witnessed the beginning of this guy’s career” - statements whose ambiguity are Sillimanian in their indeterminacy. As though, this file has not yet finished downloading, are you sure you want to open it?

Other poems like “artists decline as percentage of workforce” with its dedication of being “for and from the comment stream” continues the ideation of poem as torrent. The poem recontextualizes found language, what Wordsworth would have called the “real language of men”, and one can be sure that many of these comments are writ by men, “Michelangelo wasn’t / supported by taxpayers / and neither was da Vinci” (74). The comments are even “[fitted] to metrical arrangement” just as Wordsworth intended to. The connection here between is overdetermined of course, but this type of sampling, the poetic of harvesting comments, nonetheless taps into a discourse replete with ideological views on the role of art in society in the eyes of, perhaps, “the most important group in / the country … consumers!” (75).

This poetic is effective at being both entertaining, as it is above, but it is also effective politically: the poem “as long as you’re not doing anything wrong” dedicated to Robert Dziekanski, chops up the language used by media outlets surrounding the tragedy of his death and re-presents it scattered, torn out of its context, ready to be transparently inspected. Here again we only get blips of understanding, an incomplete translation, “look don’t behaviour you’re not clutching hours then cause won’t afraid, you exhaustion like little you your flight” (51). Here the frenetic syntax shifts constantly mimicking the disorientation of being lost in an “Int’l stapler” (51).

These poems with their blips of sense, like seeing patterns in a swarm, reminds me of Charles Bernstein’s idea of transense -

I swear he talks about it somewhere. Yes, it is a mode of translation that proposes to “transcend the divisions of national languages...an ideolect that all could understand (My Way, Speeches and Poems, 134). Except that Reimer’s poetry isn’t actually doing this - woops - but is rather always verging, skittering across and between sense. It is more like transense translated transliterally... Sense as in almost; like nonsense but not quite, “a building infested rope / in an aging residential hour / runs to almost $600 a moonbeam”, like in nonsensical, like in sonic, “drop-kick me jesus through the goalposts of wife” (34, 6). A sense that crosses between different types of sense, seeding from different types of logic, even before they finish downloading.
